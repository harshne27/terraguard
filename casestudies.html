Learning from India's Largest and Most Devastating Landslides

üî¥ Wayanad Landslide - 2024

Location: Wayanad District, Kerala

Date: July 30, 2024

Casualties: 400+ deaths, 100+ missing

Debris Volume: 6 million cubic meters

Details

This is the largest landslide in India's recorded history. Triggered by unprecedented rainfall of 572mm in just two days during the southwest monsoon, it devastated the Mundakkai and Chooralmala villages. The entire landscape was transformed as massive volumes of earth and rock descended into the valley below, completely burying settlements and destroying everything in their path. The disaster exposed the extreme vulnerability of communities in high-altitude plantation areas with fragile geological conditions.

üî¥ Kedarnath Disaster - 2013

Location: Uttarakhand Himalayas

Date: June 16-17, 2013

Casualties: 5000+ deaths confirmed, thousands missing

Affected Area: Multiple valleys and pilgrimage routes

Details

Catastrophic cloudburst and the resulting flash floods triggered hundreds of landslides. The disaster occurred during the busy pilgrimage season, with thousands of devotees trekking to the Kedarnath temple. Glacial melt combined with exceptional rainfall created perfect conditions for disaster. The scale of devastation was immense, affecting entire villages and destroying infrastructure across multiple valleys.

Lessons Learned: Importance of seasonal restrictions on pilgrimages, better forecast systems, and comprehensive disaster preparedness in pilgrimage destinations.

üî¥ Malpa Landslide - 1998

Location: Uttarakhand (Pithoragarh District)

Date: August 18, 1998

Casualties: 380+ deaths

Disaster Type: Multiple cascading slides

Details

A series of cascading landslides completely buried the village of Malpa under massive quantities of debris. The first slide triggered secondary slides that continued downslope, creating a catastrophic chain reaction. The village and its inhabitants were essentially wiped out in minutes, with the entire landscape transformed by the movement of millions of tons of earth and rock.

Lessons Learned: Understanding of cascade failures, importance of geological assessment, and need for relocation planning in vulnerable settlements.

üî¥ Pettimudi Landslide - 2020

Location: Pettimudi Valley, Idukki, Kerala

Date: August 6-7, 2020

Casualties: 70+ deaths

Affected Area: Tea plantation region

Details

A massive landslide swept through tea plantations in the Pettimudi area during the monsoon. The fragile terrain of tea plantations, combined with intensive rainfall, led to slope failure. Workers and local residents were caught off-guard by the rapid movement of debris. The landslide destroyed the plantations and scattered debris over a wide area.

Lessons Learned: Need for crop-specific risk assessment, worker safety protocols in agricultural areas, and better communication of weather alerts to rural communities.

Geographic Distribution of Case Studies

Most major landslides in India occur in the Western Ghats and Himalayan regions due to steep terrain, high rainfall, and complex geology. The Western Ghats (particularly in Kerala) and the Himalayas (Uttarakhand) are the most vulnerable regions.
